What’s The Deal With GameStop?

The GameStop Exodus

By the end of their fiscal year 2021, there were a lot of moving parts and changes within GameStop. One of the most significant changes was a change in leadership. Ryan Cohen, former CEO of Chewy until 2018, became GameStop’s new chairman of the board and CEO and pushed for an immediate shift in business strategy: to focus on scaling up their ecommerce operations and thus rebrand themselves as an ecommerce corporation for the gaming and entertainment industry.


With a new chairman of the board Ryan Cohen, among other higher management position changes, GameStop had eliminated about $314.6 million of total outstanding debt and raised about $1672.8 million in gross equity capital by the end of fiscal 2021. In other words, at some point during the fiscal 2021, GameStop had finally generated enough assets to exactly cover its obligations if the company were to be liquated and then some. However, by the end of fiscal 2022, the company’s equity greatly decreased by about 17.42% while its liabilities only 5.57%, suggesting that unless things improve, the company might fall into the same situation before 2021.

In short, the debt to equity ratio (D/E) measures the amount of debt for every dollar of equity. A firm with a high D/E ratio can indicate it is a high risk investment and vice versa. GameStop experienced a steep decrease in D/E ratio during fiscal 2021 of 74.60%. By the end of fiscal 2021, GameStop had about $1.18 of debt for every dollar of equity as opposed to $4.66 of debt per dollar of equity in fiscal 2020. This suggests that GameStop became a relatively less risky investment.

Many of these metrics are great and all for showing how GME performed during the peculiar period of fiscal 2021. But, by comparing its performance in these areas against its competitors will really show how it performed in the market and thus its overall financial health.
